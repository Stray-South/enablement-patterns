Long-form sanitized writeups of real engagements.

## Why these matter

The field-report sidebars in each pattern file (see Pattern 01 and Pattern 02) are the seed version of what lives here. A full field report is 1,500–3,000 words covering an engagement end-to-end: the situation entering, the work done, the artifacts produced, what worked, what did not, and what the experience taught about the relevant patterns.

Hiring managers and customers cite this kind of writeup as the most useful artifact in evaluating a practitioner's actual capability. It is also the rarest, because most practitioners do not write them, and the ones that do often write them in a tone that reveals more about their PR strategy than their work.

## Sanitization

The same sanitization rules apply here as in `incidents/`:

1. No client names. Operating-company names removed.
2. No specific vendor platforms.
3. No employee names.
4. Public information remains public (e.g., aggregate employee counts if publicly disclosed, regulatory regimes that publicly apply).
5. Engagement contract review before publishing.

## Format

Field reports follow a longer-form structure than pattern files:

1. **Engagement summary** — scope, scale, regulatory context, my role
2. **The problem on entry** — what the customer was trying to solve, what was working, what was failing
3. **The approach taken** — which patterns from this repo were applied and in what sequence
4. **The artifacts produced** — runbooks, registers, training programs, governance hooks
5. **What worked** — explicit, with evidence
6. **What did not work** — explicit, with mitigations
7. **What this engagement validated, invalidated, or expanded** — feedback to the patterns in this repo
8. **A closing reflection** — what I would do differently, what surprised me, what the next version of this engagement would look like

## A note on tone

Field reports in this directory are written for practitioners, not for marketing audiences. The tone is direct and honest. If something failed, it failed. If something worked, the evidence is named, not asserted.
